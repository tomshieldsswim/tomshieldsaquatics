Mental Performance · Technique

The Lion
Behind the Blocks

Tom Shields
Mental Performance
Flash Technique

There's a lion in the room right now. The blocks are up. The heat sheet is posted. The starter is about to call your event. There is a real, immediate threat to your comfort and your ego, and your body knows it.

That's stress. And stress is healthy. Your heart rate is up because you need it to be. Your body is primed because it's about to do something hard. The lion is real. Your response to it is appropriate.

Then this: It's the night before the meet. You're lying in bed. There is no lion anywhere near you. The race is over, or weeks away. Nothing is happening. And yet your brain is running the same alarm system as if the lion were standing on your chest. You're rehearsing failure. You're replaying the last bad swim. You can't let go of a thought that has no business being in the room. That's anxiety — and it's your brain manufacturing a lion that doesn't exist.

The difference between those two states is one of the most important things a competitive swimmer can learn to recognize. And it's the foundation of making my second Olympic team — a self-regulation technique called PART.

The Lineage: Where This Comes From

To understand PART, you need to know where it sits in the family tree.

It starts with Flash Technique, a clinical intervention developed by psychologist Philip Manfield, Ph.D. (Manfield et al., 2024). Flash Technique was originally designed to help people process traumatic memories — experiences that get "stuck" in the nervous system and keep triggering stress responses long after the danger has passed. The core mechanism: you briefly acknowledge a disturbing thought while simultaneously keeping your focus anchored in something positive or engaging. Your nervous system processes the difficult thing without being overwhelmed by it.

From Flash Technique, Berkeley-based marriage and family therapist Todd Harvey, MFT developed two derivatives. The first was CART (Couples Anxiety Reduction Technique) — a version where a partner provides the bilateral stimulation and emotional attunement, making the process more powerful through attachment and connection. The second was PART (Personal Activation Reduction Technique) — the solo version, designed to be done by an individual without a partner or therapist present.

PART was designed from the beginning to be self-administered — no therapist, no partner required. What I offer at camps isn't instruction or clinical guidance. It's a demonstration, based on ten years of personal experience with Todd Harvey as my therapist, of what this looks like in practice. You watch it, you try it, you take it home.

"Stress means the lion is in the room. Anxiety means your brain manufactured a lion that isn't there. PART is what you do about the manufactured one."

The Science: Why It Works

Your nervous system has two modes that matter here. The first is your sympathetic response — fight or flight, heart rate up, blood to the muscles, everything focused on the immediate threat. This is what gets activated when the starter beeps. This is exactly what you want before a race. The lion is real. Your body is doing its job.

The second is what happens when that same system fires in the absence of a real threat. The meet is next weekend. You're at dinner the night before finals. There's no lion. But your body doesn't know that, because somewhere along the way it learned to associate certain triggers — meets, blocks, heat sheets, even the smell of chlorine — with danger. Now the alarm fires preemptively. Chronically. At the worst possible times.

The mechanism behind PART — and Flash Technique — is Memory Reconsolidation Theory. When you bring a threatening thought into working memory while simultaneously flooding your system with a strong positive or resourced state, your brain has to figure out where to file that new combination. If the resourced state is strong enough, the prefrontal cortex — the rational brain — processes the threat instead of the fight-or-flight system. You become aware of the trigger rather than viscerally caught in it.

Research on this family of techniques in adolescents is encouraging. A 2023 study by Avcı found statistically significant reductions in exam anxiety in teenagers using a group Flash protocol — and the population wasn't experiencing clinical trauma. They were kids stressed about performance. That's exactly the population I work with.

What PART Actually Is

PART is not a behind-the-blocks trick. It's not a breathing cue you use in the ready room. It's a tool for when you're stuck — when anxiety has its hooks in a thought and won't let go. The 11pm spiral. The bad race you keep replaying three weeks later. The fear that keeps showing up even when nothing is actually wrong.

The protocol, as Todd designed it, works like this. You identify the thought — give it a headline, not the whole story. You don't dive into all the feelings attached to it. You just name it the way you'd name a newspaper article: "The bad relay swim." "Not being good enough." "Failing at championships." Just the title.

Then you build a resourced state — a memory, an experience, a felt sense of something good. The more vividly you can embody it, the better. You tap your chest in a slow left-right rhythm (butterfly hugs), which provides bilateral stimulation — the same basic mechanism used in EMDR. You add a soothing personal mantra, something that counteracts whatever your particular emotional raw spot is. And you add what Todd calls "V breaths" — a slow exhale that makes your diaphragm vibrate, signaling to your brain that you're safe, which starts to dial down the stress hormones.

While all of that is happening — the tapping, the mantra, the breath, the resourced state — you flutter your eyes every four or five taps. That flutter is the "flash" — a brief acknowledgment that the difficult thought exists, without actually visiting it. Your nervous system registers the threat and processes it while you stay anchored in the good thing.

You do several rounds. Then you assess: how activated are you now compared to before? Usually it's less. Often significantly less. You repeat until the thought loses its grip.

Behind the Blocks

PART isn't necessarily what you do at the blocks — some race-day stress is appropriate, and you want it. But the work you do with PART in the weeks before a big meet is exactly what determines whether you step up on race day carrying the weight of manufactured lions, or just the one that's actually there.

When the starter says "step up," something should happen in your body. Your heart should be moving. You should feel it. That feeling is your engine turning on — and if you've trained yourself to interpret it as danger, you're going to fight it, suppress it, or freeze. But if you've used tools like PART to clear out the anxiety that was never attached to anything real, that same physiological state becomes fuel instead of threat.

"The goal isn't to calm down before the race. The goal is to show up without carrying lions that don't belong there — so the one that does can do its job."

Why I Care About This

I won two Olympic gold medals. I hold American records. From the outside, my career looked like it went exactly the way it was supposed to go.

What the outside didn't see was that for a significant stretch of my career — years of it — I was in a genuinely dark place. Not in the way athletes usually talk about adversity. I mean the kind of darkness that has nothing to do with times or training and everything to do with what's happening inside when nobody's watching.

I worked through that with Todd Harvey — my therapist for ten years. He's the person who introduced me to Flash Technique, developed CART and PART, and taught me what it actually means to process something instead of just white-knuckling through it. I used these tools while I was competing at the highest level in the world. I used them during the hardest years of my life. They work.

I'm not a therapist. I want to be clear about that. If a swimmer is struggling with something serious, they need to talk to a professional — and I'll say that directly, every time. But what I can do is show PART as the self-regulation tool it was designed to be. I've lived it. And there is nothing else like it. It will not change your life overnight. But over weeks and months of simple practice — like dryland or stretching — you will see improvement.

Swimmers at every level are carrying anxiety that doesn't belong in the pool, that their coaches don't have tools to address, and that nobody taught them how to process. That's fixable. The tools exist. Most just don't know about them.

The Bottom Line

Stress is a lion in the room. The race is happening. Your body's response is appropriate — lean into it as long as it's useful. If not, PART the way.

Anxiety is your brain manufacturing a lion that isn't there. The race isn't happening yet. You can't let go of a thought. Your nervous system is firing anyway — and that is trainable.

PART is the tool. Flash Technique is the science behind it. Todd Harvey built it. I lived it. And I'm bringing it to a pool deck near you.
